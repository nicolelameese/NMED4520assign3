Walt Disney's Animated Flops

What is a Box Office Flop?

A box office flop can be described as a film that makes less money in it's opening weekend then amount of money that was put into it. Often times it is preferable for a film to create a profit so a film flops when the film does not 'break even' in the box office. For Walt Disney Animation Studios this meant that any film that cannot make its funds back is considered a flop and major changes in the Walt Disney Company happened due to many of these flops.

Why Care About Box Office Flops?

Many of these box office flop films had low audience reception, according to Rotton Tomatoes, but many of these films later on became cult classics. Addionally, I believe many of these lower rated films are meant to connect more with the youth audience than the adult's giving them their ratings.

But, What Are Walt Disney's Animated Flops?

Disney is very well known for their animated hit films such as Snow White, Cinderella, Aladdin, and more resently Frozen, Moana, and Encanto. There is so much known about the Walt Disney Company and the Walt Disney Animation Studios that we are able to get mostly accurate information to see which movies truly were box office flops and what happened as a result of these flops for the Walt Disney Studios.
Although there is more than ten of their animated films that can be considered flops, we will only focus on ten specificlly chosen ones that are the top worst for their full-length animated pictures.

Walt Disney's Biggest Animated Flops

Here is a list of 10 of Walt Disney Animation Studios' biggest box office flops. This list is in order of film age and will explore the idea that box office flops are not inherently bad movies, as many became cult classics later on. The information presented will explore the box office gross compaired to it's budget and then that will be compaired to the audience reaction according to the information from Rotton Tomatoes. Pinocchio

In 1940, just three years after the smash hit Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs they released Pinocchio, which is a darker and more mature film that was released in the midst of the Second World War, just a year before the United States of America entered the war. With of budget of $52 million, adjusted for inflation, to only make back less than $39 million. Although this film did not preform well in its initial release, it critically a masterpiece and is highly rated on Rotton Tomatoes by both the audience and professionals averging out at 84%.

Fantasia

Also released in 1940, Walt Disney's Fantasia which was a visual expirence for classical music, consisting of illistrated short stories that give emotion to the music. It was released only in special theaters for it's initial release due to theaters requiring a system called 'Fantasound' to play the film. Even though it required special care for it's release it still made nearly $43 million dollars on a $45 million dollar budget, both numbers adjusted for inflation. The critiques and audience score averaged out on Rotton Tomatoes is also very high considering it was not a box office hit, with a 89%.

Alice in Wonderland

Next big box office flop for Walt Disney Studios was Alice in Wonderland in 1951, coming off the back of mega sucess Cindarella, Alice in Wonderland was just not as well recieved during it's initial release. With a budget of 35.5 million they only made $28 million meaning their loss was nearly $10 million dollars in 1951, these numbers have been adjusted for inflation. The audience score on Rotton Tomatoes is considerably high when averaged out by the audience members reviews and the professional reviewers giving it an 81%.

Sleeping Beauty

In 1959 Disney released Sleeping Beauty, their next big princess movie after Cindarella in 1950, so they expected a large turn out, but it only made $36 million dollars in its opening week in the box office. It is believed that was the case due to the frightening look of the villan Malificient, who has later on become an anti-hero in the world of Disney. The budget of Sleeping Beauty was nearly double what it made in the initial box office at $60 million, but even though it was a poorly preforming film doesnt mean the viewers did not love and enjoy it as it's aderage score on Rotton Tomatoes is 84.5%. All of these numbers where adjusted for inflation.

Some Walt Disney History Facts!

At the times of release for these first four films there was no home movie, and television was not yet mainstreamed so these and many other Walt Disney animated films were re-released every 10 years and that was the only way for anyone to see a disney film and this is also how Disney continued to make money on the same films. Now back to the box office flops!

The Black Cauldron

In 1985, Disney released the biggest box office flop in their animated full lenght feature film history with The Black Cauldron, this film was not only a flop at the box office after a strech of good films, but it was also a flop in its story, animation, and execution as many minutes were removed from the film just weeks before its release. It is one of the lowest ranking films for Disney with a combined score of 51.5% of the reviews, and only made $53 million on a $110 million budget, numbers have been adjusted for inflation.

Some More Walt Disney History Facts!

After the invention on home video with VHS Disney decided to release their films again on home movie, but they only could get these films for a limited amount of time as they would soon be placed back into the 'Disney Vault' meaning they were no longer avalible for purchase until the next time there were out of the vault. This is a practice similar to the one from before, but instead of theatrical re-releases they were home video releases and this is how they would continue to make money on their old films, calling them classics and re-introducing the films to the next generation. Arguably this worked out very sucessfully for Disney as their older films are still some of even the younger generations favourite films.

Fantasia 2000

Following the success of the re-release of Fantasia (1940) for it's 50th anniversary Disney, specifically Walt Disney's nephew Roy E. Disney, wanted to recreate his uncle Walt's most creative film, so in 1999, Fantasia 2000 was released to commemorate not only the legacy of Walt Disney, but also the onset of the new millennium. Getting an average score of 78% on Rotton Tomatoes from its audience and reviewers and only being released in IMAX theaters for the first 6 months of its initial release, similar to the original release of Fantasia. With a budget of $135 million this film only ended up making less than $4 million dollars, the totals have been adjusted for inflation.

Treasure Planet

This 2002 film release from Walt Disney Animation Studios came right after a decade long sucess strech and was the dream film of the two directors John Musker and Ron Clements who made many beloved 90s films including, The Little Mermaid and Aladdin, that came from what is commonly known as the Disney Renaissance. This was a period of tim that was the resurgance of the Walt Disney Animated films popularity. Treasure Planet was one of the biggest budget films ot have been made at the time due to its early usage of CGI animation, costing nearly $221 million dollars to make. Sadly, this love child film only made $60 million dollars in the box office opening and has an overall rating on Rotton Tomatoes of 70.5% as later on in its life it became a cult classic to those who grew up with the film and si-fi junkies.

Home on the Range

Not long after the massive flop, Walt Disney released Home on the Range in 2004 and they spent nearly $164 million for the budget of this film, but it made less than $21 million dollars during its opening box office release. This farm animal focused film was also very poorly recieved by the audiences gettign an average ranking score of 40.5% on Rotton Tomatoes making it the worst rated Disney animated film.

Meet the Robinsons

Disney animated films slowly started becoming less and less popular due to the increasing popularity of computer animation so Disney joined the train and created this science-fiction film, but it missed all the marks of a hit film. Disney spent $211 million dollars to create this film, but only made roughly $35 million and got a mid level rating from Rotton Tomatoes of 70.5%.

Strange World

Lastly on this list is from 2022 and its Walt Disney's Strange World, which was an adorable adventure fantacy film that made obvious statments to include LBGTQ+ characters and was perhaps ahead of its time. Strange World has a budget of $180 million, but made less then $26 million in the box office opening week. Perhaps people became framiliar with Disney's next pattern of a theatrical release followed by an online streaming release 30 days later on their Disney+ online streaming platform for free with a subscription, so people simply decided to just wait a month to see the film and the average review on Rotton Tomatoes is only 69%.
